Journey through time: 3000 BCE - 500 CE

3000 BCE - 500 CE
3100 BCE

Unification of Upper and Lower Egypt

King Menes (Narmer) unites Egypt

King Menes, also known as Narmer, united Upper and Lower Egypt, establishing the First Dynasty and beginning the Early Dynastic Period. This marked the birth of one of the world's first great civilizations.

Politics Early Dynastic Period
2560 BCE

Great Pyramid of Giza Completed

Built for Pharaoh Khufu

The Great Pyramid, built as a tomb for Pharaoh Khufu, was the tallest man-made structure in the world for over 3,800 years. It demonstrates the Egyptians' advanced knowledge of mathematics, astronomy, and engineering.

Architecture Old Kingdom
1332 BCE

Tutankhamun Becomes Pharaoh

The boy king ascends to the throne

Tutankhamun became pharaoh at the age of nine. Though his reign was brief, his nearly intact tomb discovered in 1922 provided unprecedented insight into royal life and burial practices of ancient Egypt.

Politics New Kingdom
776 BCE

First Olympic Games

Athletic competitions in honor of Zeus

The first recorded Olympic Games were held in Olympia, Greece. Athletes from various Greek city-states competed in events including running, wrestling, boxing, and chariot racing. The games were held every four years and were so important that wars were suspended during the competition.

Culture Archaic Period
508 BCE

Democracy in Athens

Cleisthenes introduces democratic reforms

Cleisthenes established what is generally considered the first democratic system of government. His reforms included creating the Assembly (Ekklesia) where citizens could vote on laws and policies, introducing the concept of equal political rights (isonomia).

Politics Classical Period
432 BCE

Completion of the Parthenon

Temple dedicated to Athena on the Acropolis

The Parthenon, designed by architects Ictinus and Callicrates under the supervision of sculptor Phidias, was completed. This magnificent temple embodied the ideals of harmony, proportion, and mathematical precision that characterized Greek architecture.

Architecture Classical Period
753 BCE

Founding of Rome

Legendary founding by Romulus and Remus

According to legend, Rome was founded by twin brothers Romulus and Remus, who were raised by a she-wolf. After a dispute, Romulus killed Remus and named the city after himself. While historically dubious, this date marks the traditional founding of what would become one of history's greatest empires.

Foundation Kingdom Period
44 BCE

Assassination of Julius Caesar

Caesar killed by senators on the Ides of March

Julius Caesar was assassinated by a group of senators led by Marcus Junius Brutus and Gaius Cassius Longinus. His death marked the end of the Roman Republic and the beginning of the Roman Empire under his adopted son Octavian (later Augustus).

Politics Late Republic
80 CE

Completion of the Colosseum

Rome's iconic amphitheater opens

The Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, was completed under Emperor Titus. It could hold up to 80,000 spectators who came to watch gladiatorial contests, animal hunts, executions, and dramatic performances. It remains one of the most impressive examples of Roman engineering and architecture.

Architecture Early Empire
395 CE

Division of the Roman Empire

Empire splits into Eastern and Western halves

Upon the death of Emperor Theodosius I, the Roman Empire was permanently divided into Eastern and Western halves. The Eastern Roman Empire (later known as the Byzantine Empire) would survive for another thousand years, while the Western Roman Empire would fall in 476 CE.

Politics Late Empire
